The Red Herring Fallacy exists because most political arguments are not actually about what they claim to be about. The debate about crime is about race. The debate about gender is about power. The debate about free speech is about who gets to speak without consequence. Every major public argument has a misdirection at its centre — a red herring — and most of us are too busy being outraged to notice we've already been moved away from the real question.

This publication is an attempt to name that. Directly. Without performing balance where there isn't any, and without pretending that rigour and anger are mutually exclusive.

It covers feminist theory, true crime, politics, sociology, gender and sexuality, philosophy of argument, race and identity, and law and justice — not as separate silos but as a single, interconnected question: whose argument is this serving, and what are they not saying?

About the Editor

Tshegofatso Mamphoko is a South African writer and the founder of The Red Herring Fallacy. She writes about power: who holds it, how it is justified, and what gets obscured in the process. Direct by instinct, rigorous by necessity, and constitutionally incapable of leaving a bad argument unchallenged.
