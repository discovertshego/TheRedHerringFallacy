The Jesters Have Always Known

On celebrity, moral distraction, the Epstein network, and the grotesque hierarchy of acceptable sin.

By Tshegofatso Mamphoko · March 24, 2026 · 10 min read

Your Show Must Go On

Pots on a stove — every one of them boils over fast and cools down even faster. That is the nature of public outrage. It scalds on contact and evaporates before you can decide what to do with it. What I have come to understand about rumours, controversies, and the machinery that produces them is that their velocity is the point. News travels fast. The intensity doubles when the name attached has a face we collectively know, love, or have decided to hate.

Celebrities exist at a particular intersection of intimacy and spectacle. Their lives are placed on public display — not incidentally, not reluctantly, but as the primary transaction. We call it entertainment. What it actually is, is surveillance with consent, an arrangement in which a person agrees to be watched in exchange for wealth, influence, and the peculiar kind of immortality that comes from being a cultural reference point. We, in turn, call our participation in this arrangement fanning, stanning, keeping up, and lately, cancelling. It has the texture of engagement. It functions more like a leash — on both sides.

Someone once said celebrities are the original jesters of our generation. The observation is more precise than it sounds. The jester, in the medieval court, occupied a position that was simultaneously marginal and essential. They were permitted — required, even — to mock authority, to speak uncomfortable truths in the register of absurdity, to puncture the self-importance of the powerful while remaining firmly beneath them in the social order. They were licensed fools. Their function was not purely comedic. It was structural: they absorbed social tension, redirected dissent into laughter, and in doing so, kept the court intact. The court kept them employed. Everyone understood the terms.

The philosopher Mikhail Bakhtin, writing on carnival and the grotesque in Rabelais and His World (1965), described the jester's tradition as a form of sanctioned transgression — a release valve that, paradoxically, reinforced the very hierarchies it appeared to challenge. The laughter was real. The subversion was managed. The system remained.

We never stopped needing jesters. We just gave them better lighting.

The Meat Dress as Proof of Concept

On September 12, 2010, Lady Gaga arrived at the MTV Video Music Awards wearing a dress constructed entirely of raw beef. At the time, the response ranged from fascinated to repulsed to reverential. She was called provocateur, artist, madwoman. The dress was discussed, debated, and eventually installed in the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame in a freeze-dried form. It became iconic the way things become iconic when they are sufficiently strange and sufficiently repeated.

I remember the feeling of it — wild, daring, almost confrontational in its excess. When I think about it now, I think about it differently. Not because the dress was sinister, but because of what it was part of: a long sequence of escalating provocation in which the question being asked, implicitly, was not "what do you think of this?" but rather "how much will you accept?" Each push was a calibration. Each moment of public normalisation was data. The threshold for shock was being mapped in real time, and we were the test subjects, and we volunteered enthusiastically.

This is not a conspiracy in the conventional sense. It does not require a room full of people agreeing on a plan. It requires only the logic of the attention economy, pursued to its conclusion: find the edge of acceptable, then move the edge. Repeat. The process is self-reinforcing because the audience is always hungry for the next thing, and the next thing must always be more. More extreme, more confessional, more transgressive, more intimate. The appetite is never satisfied because satisfaction is not the design.

Epstein, and the Machinery of Distraction

Jeffrey Epstein was arrested in July 2019 on federal charges of sex trafficking minors. He was found dead in his cell in August of that year under circumstances that a significant portion of the public — and some forensic experts — found inconsistent with suicide. The names in his orbit included politicians, financiers, royalty, and entertainment figures across multiple continents. The files associated with his case have been released partially, contested, delayed, and redacted in ways that have kept the story in a permanent state of half-disclosure.

What is notable is not just the content of what has been disclosed. It is the rhythm of the disclosure. Each release arrives in a moment already saturated with competing demands on public attention — a war, an election, an economic crisis, a celebrity implosion. The files surface, the internet erupts briefly, and then something else occupies the feed. The boiling pot cools. The next pot begins to heat.

An audio clip circulated in early 2025 — widely claimed to be a recording of a sitting president instructing aides to suppress the Epstein files at any cost, including the cost of military escalation. Whether the audio is genuine or fabricated, it captures something true about the epistemological position we now occupy: we no longer know how to distinguish the real from the constructed, and the people who benefit from that confusion are not interested in helping us resolve it. Deepfakes are useful precisely because they make real things deniable. Once the technology exists, everything is deniable. Every document becomes forgeable in retrospect.

This is what I mean when I say I spend time questioning the validity of our running institutions. Not because I have concluded they are entirely fraudulent — though some are — but because the tools we would use to test them have been systematically compromised. The philosopher Onora O'Neill, in her 2002 Reith Lectures on trust, argued that trust requires the ability to verify — that without access to honest, independent information, trust collapses not into distrust but into something more corrosive: the suspension of judgment altogether. We stop asking whether something is true. We ask only whether it is useful or entertaining.

"Trust requires the ability to verify. Without honest, independent information, we don't become distrustful — we become incapable of judgment altogether." — Onora O'Neill, A Question of Trust, BBC Reith Lectures, 2002

What We Mean When We Say Humanity

The question underneath all of this is the oldest one: how far have we come, as a species, and at what cost? The word humanity carries two meanings that are in constant tension with each other. The first is taxonomic — humanity as the collective noun for human beings, the species, the sum of us. The second is moral — humanity as a quality, a disposition toward compassion, dignity, and the recognition of others as fully real.

Immanuel Kant, in the Groundwork for the Metaphysics of Morals (1785), articulated the second meaning through what he called the Formula of Humanity: act so that you treat humanity, whether in your own person or that of another, always as an end and never merely as a means. The prohibition is specific. Not that people should never be used — all social life involves some degree of instrumentalisation. But that they should never be used merely as means, never reduced entirely to their utility, never stripped of the inner life that makes them more than a resource.

By that standard, the question of how far we have come is uncomfortable. We have built extraordinary systems of knowledge, medicine, communication, and art. We have also built systems of surveillance, extraction, and spectacle that treat entire categories of people as raw material — for labour, for content, for experiment, for gratification. The two projects have proceeded in parallel, and the distance between them has not narrowed as much as our self-congratulation suggests.

The Grotesque Hierarchy of Acceptable Sin

There is a particular incoherence that runs through public moral discourse that I find it necessary to name directly. It concerns the disproportionate, culturally sanctioned hostility directed at homosexuality — specifically gay men — relative to the relative silence, minimisation, or active protection extended to those who sexually abuse children.

This is not a new observation, but it is one that the Epstein case makes newly urgent. The network that surrounded Epstein was not a network of gay men. It was a network of powerful, predominantly heterosexual men who trafficked children. The documented victims were girls. The perpetrators and their associates have, in many cases, continued to occupy positions of influence, attend galas, appear on panels, and receive the ambient social protection that wealth provides. Some have faced consequences. Many have not.

Meanwhile, in legislative chambers across multiple countries — including South Africa, across sub-Saharan Africa, and in American states with active culture war agendas — the energy directed at restricting, criminalising, and demonising same-sex relationships remains remarkable in its consistency and ferocity. People who would never extend a public opinion on the documented abuse of minors by powerful men have strong, loudly held views on who consenting adults may love.

The research literature on this overlap is worth engaging. A 2002 study published in the Archives of Sexual Behavior by Freund and Watson found no meaningful correlation between homosexual orientation and attraction to minors — the two categories are empirically distinct. The conflation is not a mistake born of ignorance. It is a rhetorical strategy, one that redirects disgust toward a socially vulnerable group while insulating a socially powerful one.

What we are watching, when we watch this dynamic, is Bakhtin's sanctioned transgression in its most cynical form. The jester points at the queer person. The court laughs and feels righteous. The actual harm — the child in the room that nobody is naming — goes unaddressed because addressing it would require looking at the people who run the court.

The Show Must Go On

The curtain in the image that opens this piece reads: Your show must go on. It is the most honest thing the entertainment industry has ever said about itself. The show is not incidental to the structure. The show is the structure. It is what keeps the audience seated while the decisions that affect their lives are made in rooms they will never enter, by people whose names they may never know.

I do not write this from a position of pure cynicism. I write it from a position of informed frustration — the kind that comes not from hopelessness but from having looked carefully at the machinery and understood how it works. Understanding how something works is the precondition for changing it. The jester who knows they are a jester is a different animal from the one who believes in the laughter.

The meat dress was a calibration. The Epstein files are a suppression. The moral panic about gay people is a misdirection. The distraction is the product. And the audience — us, sitting here, in the glow of our feeds, our outrage cycling through its regular rhythms of boiling and cooling — we are not outside the show.

We are the show.
