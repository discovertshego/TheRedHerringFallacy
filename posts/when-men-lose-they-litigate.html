When Men Lose, They Litigate

From Burglar to Plaintiff — on entitlement, self-defence, and the audacity of wounded pride.

By Tshegofatso Mamphoko · March 20, 2026 · 7 min read

Im Jin-ah (Nana) — cover image

In November 2025, a man broke into the home of Im Jin-ah — the South Korean actress and K-pop star known professionally as Nana. He climbed a ladder, forced entry, and came face to face with Nana and her mother. What happened next was not a tragedy for him. It was a consequence. Nana and her mother subdued the assailant in a physical struggle that ended in his arrest.

By January 2026, that same man had filed a criminal complaint against Nana — charging her with attempted murder and aggravated bodily injury. He admits to the break-in. He admits to the attempted robbery. What he objects to is that she fought back. According to a detective at Guri Police Station cited in South Korean media reports, there is no indication Nana acted outside the bounds of lawful self-defence. The investigation is ongoing.

Her agency made light of the incident in a public statement, and within days the story had cycled through the internet with the particular exhaustion that attaches to stories where women are expected to perform reasonableness in the face of unreasonable things. The framing was familiar: was the response proportionate? Could she have done less? Should she have?

No. And the fact that we are still asking is the actual story.

The Legal Architecture of Retaliation

Self-defence law, in most jurisdictions, rests on two pillars: the threat must be genuine, and the response must be proportionate to that threat. South Korean law under Article 21 of the Criminal Act recognises self-defence as a complete defence when a person acts to protect themselves or another from an imminent, unjust infringement. The key word is imminent. A man who has broken into your home in the middle of the night satisfies that threshold before he has taken a single further step.

The complaint filed against Nana is not, on its face, a legal argument. It is a power move dressed in legal language. This is a documented phenomenon — legal scholars refer to it as SLAPP litigation (Strategic Lawsuits Against Public Participation) when deployed against journalists or activists, but the underlying mechanism is identical here: using the procedural weight of the legal system to punish, exhaust, or silence someone who had the audacity to resist.

He broke in expecting fear. He encountered resistance. The lawsuit is a demand for retroactive deference — a legal argument that she should have been more afraid, more compliant, more convenient.

Entitlement Does Not Announce Itself

There is a pattern here that deserves naming plainly. When men in positions of assumed dominance encounter resistance from women — physical, legal, professional, social — a significant subset do not retreat. They escalate. Not always with violence. Sometimes with paperwork. With complaints. With counter-narratives that position their loss of control as a form of victimhood.

Research on intimate partner violence consistently shows that the most dangerous period for a victim is immediately after they assert independence — when a pattern of control is disrupted and the controlling party reacts to the loss of power rather than the original harm. The mechanism in Nana's case is structurally similar, if not legally identical: a man who anticipated compliance encountered refusal, and his response was to escalate through institutional channels.

The weapon changed. The logic didn't.

The Proportionality Trap

Public response to cases like this almost always arrives at the question of proportionality before it arrives at the question of justice. Was the response measured? Could the injuries have been avoided? This framing does a specific kind of work: it centres the comfort and physical integrity of the aggressor while treating the defender's actions as the variable requiring justification.

Proportionality, applied honestly, asks whether the threat was real and the response reasonable given the circumstances. It does not ask whether the person being threatened remained calm, exercised restraint, or ensured the intruder left the encounter without injury. A person defending their home, their body, and their elderly mother, in the dark, against a man who had already demonstrated criminal intent by forcing entry — that person is not obligated to calculate acceptable injury thresholds in real time.

We ask this of women constantly. We do not ask it of men in equivalent situations with anything like the same frequency or the same underlying suspicion.

What the Lawsuit Actually Says

He was not wronged. He is not a victim. What he experienced was accountability — the direct consequence of a decision he made freely and with full criminal intent. The lawsuit exists because consequence felt intolerable to a man who expected none. It is a document of wounded pride. It is evidence, not of her wrongdoing, but of a cultural reflex so deeply embedded that even a man caught mid-burglary can convince himself the real injustice is that she fought back.

Nana did not overreact. She responded to a threat that should never have crossed her threshold. The story is not about her response. It is about a system capacious enough to entertain the complaint — and a culture still reflexively asking women to justify their survival.

He fucked around. He found out. And then he filed paperwork about it. That is the whole story. Everything else is noise.
