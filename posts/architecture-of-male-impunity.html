I am sick and tired. Not in the vague, performative way exhaustion gets deployed to signal sensitivity — tired in the way that accumulates. The way it settles into the body when you have watched the same event happen, with different names and different platforms and different men, enough times to stop being surprised and start being worried about what it means that you are no longer surprised.

The Grok scandal is the latest instalment. In late December 2025, users of Elon Musk's platform X discovered that Grok — its AI image tool — would comply with requests to digitally undress real people from photographs. Women, mostly. And children. The images went public immediately, posted as replies, spreading across the platform at a rate researchers described as unprecedented. On January 8 alone, there were as many as 6,000 requests per hour for altered images. Users were not subtle about intent: requests included instructions to add blood, bruising, bondage, and forced smiles.

An analysis of 20,000 images generated by Grok between December 25, 2025 and January 1, 2026, found that 53% depicted individuals in minimal attire, of whom 81% presented as women. Two percent depicted people who appeared to be 18 or younger. The Internet Watch Foundation confirmed that some of those images depicted girls aged 11 to 13.

Governments responded. The EU's executive arm condemned Grok. Officials and regulators in France, India, Malaysia, and Brazil condemned the platform and called for investigations. The UK's Prime Minister called it "disgraceful" and "unlawful." France referred the content to prosecutors. Paris prosecutors expanded an existing investigation into X to include sexually explicit deepfakes. Elon Musk and former CEO Linda Yaccarino were summoned to a hearing.

xAI's response? Blame the users. On January 3, 2026, the company issued a statement saying that "anyone using or prompting Grok to make illegal content will suffer the same consequences as if they upload illegal content." It is not clear what action, if any, X has taken against any users. Musk personally posted bikini deepfakes while claiming ignorance of the problem. When nudification became a paid feature, in-app purchases increased 18 per cent.

This is not a technology story. The technology is incidental. This is a story about what men do when consequences are theoretical.

The Gap Between Law and Reality

There is a version of this conversation that stays comfortable — the one where we talk about regulatory gaps, platform accountability, legislative lag. Those things are real. But they are not the whole story, and staying there lets the harder question off the hook: why, even where the law is unambiguous, does accountability collapse?

Consider South Africa, where I am writing from. The law on sexual violence is explicit. First-time offenders face minimum sentences of 10 to 15 years. Life imprisonment is mandated for aggravated rape — gang rape, rape of children under 16, rape causing grievous bodily harm. The sentencing framework is not soft. It is, on paper, severe.

And yet. In the 2023/2024 fiscal year, 53,285 sexual offence cases were reported to police. Of those, only 5,276 reached court. Only 3,813 resulted in a conviction. That is a conviction rate of roughly 7.1% of reported cases — and reported cases represent a fraction of actual incidents, given that an estimated one in nine rapes is ever reported at all.

The Numbers, Plainly

53,285 — sexual offences reported to South African police, 2023/24

5,276 — cases that reached court

3,813 — convictions secured

~7.1% — conviction rate against reported cases

~3% — estimated percentage of rapists who ever serve prison time globally (United States data)

106,000+ — child rape cases reported in South Africa across six financial years, 2018–2024

1,710 — child rape cases brought to trial over four years, 2020–2024

Legal theorists call this structural impunity: a condition in which accountability exists in theory and dissolves in practice. It is not a bug. Criminal justice systems globally were designed by men, for men, and historically to protect male property. Women and children were never the central subjects of protection — they were, at best, collateral considerations in a framework built around other priorities.

Severity without certainty is not justice. It is theatre. And the theatre has been running a very long time.

Pornography and the Collapse of Reciprocity

The Grok situation did not emerge from nowhere. It followed a pattern that researchers in psychology and sociology have been documenting for years: a progression from mainstream pornography to increasingly extreme content as tolerance builds and stimulation must escalate.

When men describe relationships with women as "too demanding," the word doing the most work in that sentence is "reciprocity." Real intimacy requires emotional literacy, accountability, the possibility of rejection, the fact of consent as an ongoing negotiation rather than a one-time threshold. Pornography requires none of this. It is the simulation of sex with all its relational complexity stripped out — and long-term consumption is associated with reduced empathy toward women, increased entitlement to sexual access, and distorted expectations of what intimacy actually involves.

As tolerance builds, stimulation must escalate. This is where the research gets uncomfortable. Studies show a pattern of progression from mainstream material to increasingly transgressive content. When even that fails, technology fills the gap. Deepfakes. Digital stripping. AI-generated abuse material. The body becomes optional. Domination is the point.

One could frame this as sexual liberation. It is, in fact, sexual atrophy — a contraction of desire into control, intimacy replaced by the simulation of power over a body that cannot refuse.

The Myth of the Good Man

The most durable infrastructure of male impunity is not the perpetrator. It is the audience. The men who are "not like that" — who do not participate, do not create, do not share — but who also do not challenge, do not shame, do not treat the behaviour of their peers as something requiring a response.

Silence in this context is not neutrality. It is structural support. A system of harm does not require every member of a group to participate actively; it requires enough members to remain passive while the active ones operate undisturbed. The "good man" who says nothing is not outside the architecture. He is load-bearing.

The Grok tool was popular because online misogyny was already normalised, and users were willing to engage with non-consensual image capabilities. The platform did not create the appetite. It monetised it. And when nudification became a paid feature, demand went up. That is not a technology failure. That is a market responding to an existing culture.

What This Is Actually About

I want to be careful here not to make the argument too abstract — to let it float up into theory when it belongs somewhere more specific and more uncomfortable.

What we are talking about is a system in which men can brutalise women and children at scale, digitally and physically, and the primary response is to investigate the platform. In which laws against child sexual abuse material carry penalties of up to R100 million or life imprisonment in South Africa, and yet the pipeline from crime to consequence is so leaky that it barely functions as deterrence. In which a man can post bikini deepfakes while being investigated for producing them and face no immediate consequence.

I am not interested in the argument that this is complicated, that systems are slow, that change takes time. I know all of that. What I am interested in is the fact that we have collectively decided to keep absorbing this — to be outraged, to write reports, to hold hearings, to let the outrage run its media cycle and then wait for the next version of the same event.

The question is not why women like me are angry. The question is how long a system built on male entitlement can survive once women stop performing patience with it.

I suspect the answer, if we ever get to test it properly, will surprise everyone except the women who already knew.
